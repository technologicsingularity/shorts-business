How QR Codes Actually Work (30% Can Be Destroyed)

Did you know a QR code can have 30% of it destroyed and still work perfectly? That logo in the center? It's covering actual data. Yet your phone reads it instantly. Here's the secret.

QR stands for Quick Response. Those three squares in the corners? Position markers. Your phone uses them to figure out which way is up, even if you rotate the code.

The black and white pattern? That's binary data — ones and zeros. But here's where it gets wild. QR codes use something called Reed-Solomon error correction. It's the same technology used on CDs and spacecraft.

The code actually contains extra data — redundant information spread throughout. So if part gets smudged, scratched, or covered by a logo, the missing data can be mathematically reconstructed from what's left.

There are four levels. Level L handles 7% damage. Level M handles 15%. Level Q handles 25%. And Level H? A whopping 30% of the code can be destroyed and your phone still reads it.

That logo in the center is basically vandalism that the code is designed to survive. Pretty clever, right?
