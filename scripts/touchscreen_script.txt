Title: How Touchscreens Actually Work (It's Not What You Touch)

Every time you tap your phone, something invisible happens. Your finger never actually touches the screen—not really.

Your screen has a grid of transparent electrodes, thinner than a human hair, embedded in glass. When your finger gets close—within a few millimeters—it changes the electrical field at that exact spot.

The phone detects this disturbance at multiple points and calculates exactly where your finger is. All of this happens in milliseconds, thousands of times per second.

But here's the wild part: it works through your skin's conductivity. That means gloves block it, unless they're specially designed. The screen is literally reading your body's electrical signature.

So next time you scroll, remember—you're not touching technology. You're conducting electricity through glass, and your phone is watching the invisible field you create. That's how touchscreens actually work.
